Welcome to the Archive Foundation

The Archive Foundation is a global non-profit organization dedicated to the identification, documentation, and containment of potentially harmful information - known as infohazards - to preserve public safety and protect the collective knowledge of humanity.

Established in 1952 following several documented cases of information-based harm, our organization works closely with governments, academic institutions, and private enterprises worldwide to develop protocols for safely handling dangerous knowledge.

OUR MISSION

The mission of the Archive Foundation is threefold:

  1. Identify information with the potential to cause harm if improperly accessed or understood
  2. Develop containment protocols appropriate to the level of risk
  3. Educate the public on safe information handling practices

We believe that knowledge itself is neutral, but the context, dissemination, and application of certain types of information may pose significant risks to individuals or society. Our work ensures that beneficial knowledge can be preserved while mitigating potential harms.

WHAT ARE INFOHAZARDS?

An infohazard is any information that poses a risk of harm to the recipient, others, or society at large. Unlike physical hazards, infohazards operate through cognitive mechanisms and can spread through communication channels including the Hypernet.

Categories of infohazards include:

  • Memetic Hazards: Information that causes harmful changes to beliefs or behaviors
  • Instructional Hazards: Information that enables harmful activities
  • Dimensional Anomalies: Information related to spacetime irregularities
  • Perception-Altering Hazards: Information that disrupts normal sensory processing
  • Existential Hazards: Information that poses risks to human civilization

The Archive Foundation uses a classification system to categorize infohazards by type, severity, and containment requirements.

INFOHAZARD REGISTRY

The following is a curated list of publicly acknowledged infohazards. For security reasons, detailed information about high-level threats has been redacted or secured behind appropriate clearance requirements.

Taos Dimensional Rift (1996)
Classification: Type DA-3 (Dimensional Anomaly, Level 3)
Information pertaining to the unexplained dimensional phenomenon that appeared near Taos, New Mexico in August 1996. Witnesses reported seeing "impossible geometries" and experiencing unusual perceptual effects. While the anomaly itself collapsed after approximately four hours, information about its specific visual characteristics may induce mild perceptual distortions in sensitive individuals. See Sanitized Public Report.
The Recursive Equation
Classification: Type CH-2 (Cognitive Hazard, Level 2)
A mathematical formula discovered in 1998 that, when comprehended fully, leads to obsessive calculation behaviors and temporary disruption of normal thought patterns. The equation itself has been classified, but educational materials about its effects are available to qualified researchers with Level 2 clearance or higher.
The Weeping Frequency
Classification: Type AH-2 (Auditory Hazard, Level 2)
A specific audio frequency pattern that induces strong emotional responses, particularly uncontrollable crying. Originally discovered during telecommunications research in 1987. Digital representations of this frequency are monitored on the Hypernet and automatically filtered by most ISPs in compliance with Foundation guidelines.

The Stockholm Manuscript
Classification: Type MH-3 (Memetic Hazard, Level 3)
A 16th-century text containing linguistic patterns that alter readers' perception of time, leading to disorientation and in some cases, temporary amnesia. Original text is secured in Foundation archives, with research access granted only under controlled conditions. A sanitized analysis is available through our academic publications.

REPORTING POTENTIAL INFOHAZARDS

The Archive Foundation relies on vigilant citizens to report potentially harmful information encountered on the Hypernet or in other media. If you believe you have encountered an infohazard:

  1. Do NOT share or describe the information to others
  2. Document where you encountered it (URL, book, etc.)
  3. Contact our secure reporting line: 1-800-INFO-SEC
  4. Alternatively, submit a report through our secure reporting form
IMPORTANT: If you experience unusual symptoms after exposure to suspected infohazardous material, please consult our emergency protocols immediately.

All reports are confidential and protected under international information security agreements.

RESEARCH INITIATIVES

The Archive Foundation supports numerous research initiatives aimed at better understanding and containing infohazards. Current projects include:

  • Development of improved memetic inoculation techniques
  • Cognitive resilience training programs for at-risk populations
  • Automated detection of hazardous information patterns on the Hypernet
  • Dimensional stability monitoring following the 1996 Taos Incident
  • Information hygiene best practices for educational institutions

Qualified researchers interested in contributing to these initiatives should review our research opportunities page.
