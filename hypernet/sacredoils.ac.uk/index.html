Welcome to the Archive

This repository documents the historical use of sacred oils in necromantic practices across ancient civilizations. Our research focuses on archaeological findings, manuscript translations, and anthropological studies dating from antiquity through 1001 CE.

The use of consecrated oils in death rites and spirit communion was a cornerstone of ancient mystery religions. From the Egyptian kyphi to the Mesopotamian ritual unguents, these substances served as both physical and metaphysical conduits between the realm of the living and the deceased.

Current Research Focus

• Translation of the Codex Oleorum Mortis (discovered 1987)

• Analysis of oil residues from Etruscan burial sites

• Comparative study of medieval European necromantic texts

• Chemical composition of ancient Egyptian embalming oils

Our work contributes to the broader understanding of ancient religious practices and the role of material culture in spiritual beliefs. All research is conducted in accordance with archaeological ethics and cultural sensitivity guidelines.

Classification of Sacred Oils

Based on manuscript evidence and archaeological analysis, we have catalogued various categories of ritual oils used in necromantic practices:

Kyphi Nectaris Egyptian Soul binding Frankincense, wine, honey
Unguentum Umbrae Celtic/Druidic Shadow work Animal fat, nightshade, iron filings

Preparation Methods

Ancient texts describe elaborate preparation rituals that could take lunar months to complete. The oils were typically consecrated during specific astronomical events and stored in vessels made from materials associated with the underworld - lead, obsidian, or blackened clay.

Chemical analysis has revealed complex organic compounds in archaeological samples, including traces of psychoactive substances that may have induced altered states of consciousness during ritual use.

Ancient Ritual Practices

Historical accounts describe various ceremonial applications of sacred oils in necromantic rites. These practices were integral to ancient death cults and mystery religions.

The Rite of the Descending Flame

Source: Papyrus Necromanticus (Alexandria, 3rd century CE)

Purpose: Communication with recently deceased

Oil Used: Oleum Mortuum mixed with wax and ground pearls

Method: Oil was burned in bronze censers while practitioners entered meditative trances. The smoke was believed to carry messages to the underworld.

The Anointing of Threshold Guardians

Source: Etruscan tomb inscriptions (6th century BCE)

Purpose: Protection during spirit communication

Oil Used: Sesamum Mortalis with crushed obsidian

Method: Doorways and ritual implements were anointed to create barriers against malevolent entities.

The Bath of Shadows

Source: Celtic oral traditions (recorded 9th century)

Purpose: Spiritual purification before major workings

Oil Used: Unguentum Umbrae diluted in spring water

Method: Practitioners bathed in the oil mixture during the dark moon to cleanse astral impurities.

Archaeological evidence suggests these practices were widespread across the ancient Mediterranean and Northern European cultures, with regional variations reflecting local beliefs about death and the afterlife.

Primary Source Documents

Our archive contains translations and analyses of key historical texts documenting necromantic oil use:

The Codex Oleorum Mortis

Date: 8th century CE (estimated)

Language: Medieval Latin with Greek annotations

Location: Monastery of San Benedetto, Italy

Contents: 47 folios detailing oil preparation, ritual timing, and safety precautions for necromantic practices.

Papyrus Necromanticus

Date: 3rd century CE

Language: Coptic with hieroglyphic passages

Location: Alexandria Museum archives

Contents: Fragment describing the "Rite of the Descending Flame" and related oil formulations.

The Book of Buried Flames

Date: 12th century CE

Language: Old Irish with Latin glosses

Location: Trinity College Dublin

Contents: Celtic druidic traditions adapted for Christian monastery use, including oil consecration methods.

All translations are ongoing projects involving collaboration with medieval studies departments at major universities. We welcome scholarly correspondence regarding these documents.

About the Archive

The Sacred Oils Archive was established in 1995 as a collaborative project between the Department of Archaeology at Cambridge University and the Institute for Medieval Studies at Oxford. Our mission is to preserve and study historical evidence of ritual oil use in ancient necromantic practices.
