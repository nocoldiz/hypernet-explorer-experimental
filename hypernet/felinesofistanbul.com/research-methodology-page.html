Introduction to Feline Anthropology

Since 1990, I have dedicated myself to the systematic documentation of Istanbul's feline population, developing what I term "feline anthropology" - the study of cats within their cultural and urban contexts. Unlike traditional zoological approaches that might examine cats primarily as biological entities, my methodology places equal emphasis on the cultural, historical, and social dimensions of feline existence within the specific context of Istanbul.

This page outlines the rigorous research methods I have developed and refined over the past decade. While some colleagues from traditional academic disciplines have questioned the formal structure of my approach, I maintain that the study of urban cats requires specialized techniques that bridge multiple disciplines including anthropology, zoology, history, and urban studies.

"The study of urban cats cannot be confined to a single academic discipline. These creatures exist at the intersection of nature and culture, of wild and domestic, of past and present. Their proper documentation requires methodological innovation and interdisciplinary thinking."
- From my research journal, March 1997

The methods described below have been developed specifically for the Istanbul context but may be adapted by other researchers studying feline populations in different urban environments. I welcome correspondence from fellow researchers interested in applying these techniques to their own local cat communities.

Core Research Principles

My approach to feline documentation is guided by several fundamental principles that have remained consistent throughout my research career:

1. Non-Interventionist Observation

I maintain a strict policy of minimal intervention in the natural behaviors and territories of the cats I study. Unlike laboratory research that places animals in artificial conditions, my approach emphasizes observing cats in their natural urban environment with minimal disruption to their normal activities.

This principle occasionally requires declining to assist cats that appear to be in mild distress, as intervention would compromise the integrity of behavioral observation. However, in cases of serious injury or illness, ethical considerations take precedence over research protocols.

2. Long-Term Longitudinal Documentation

Rather than conducting short-term studies with large sample sizes, I emphasize extended observation of specific individual cats over periods of months or years. This longitudinal approach allows for documentation of seasonal behavioral variations, territory shifts, social relationship development, and individual life histories.

For example, my documentation of Whisker Pasha spans over 26 months at the time of this writing, with observation notes collected in all seasons and weather conditions, providing a comprehensive picture of his behavior patterns and life cycle.

3. Cultural Context Integration

I deliberately integrate historical and cultural context into my feline observations, recognizing that Istanbul's cats cannot be properly understood in isolation from the human society with which they coexist. This includes documenting human-cat interactions, architectural accommodations for cats, and the cultural traditions that shape attitudes toward urban felines.

This principle distinguishes my work from purely biological approaches and places it within the broader framework of cultural studies and urban anthropology.

4. Methodological Transparency

I maintain meticulous records of my research activities, including observation hours, weather conditions, equipment used, and potential observational biases. This transparency allows other researchers to evaluate the reliability of my findings and potentially replicate my methods in other contexts.

All field notes are dated and cross-referenced, with clear distinction between direct observations and inferences or conclusions drawn from these observations.

"The street cats of Istanbul are not merely biological organisms but cultural entities, shaped by centuries of human-feline coexistence. To study them without reference to this cultural context would be to miss their essential nature."
- From "Nine Lives, Nine Centuries," my 1998 monograph

Observation Protocol

During each observation session, I follow a structured protocol:

  1. Initial Survey (15 minutes) - Upon arriving at the observation location, I conduct a general survey of the area, noting environmental conditions, human activity levels, and presence of any cats in the vicinity.
  2. Subject Location (variable time) - If observing a specific cat subject, I locate the individual using knowledge of their typical territory and habits. If conducting a general population study, I select a strategic observation point with good visibility.
  3. Continuous Observation (minimum 45 minutes) - Once the subject is located, I observe continuously for at least 45 minutes, noting all activities, interactions, and movements in my field journal. I use standardized codes for common behaviors to ensure consistency.
  4. Context Documentation (15 minutes) - After the continuous observation period, I document relevant contextual factors including weather, human activities in the area, food sources, and interactions with other animals.
  5. Photography (as appropriate) - When conditions permit and the subject's behavior won't be significantly altered, I take photographs to document physical characteristics, behaviors, or environmental features. All photographs are labeled with date, time, and location information.

Behavioral Coding System

To ensure consistency in my field notes, I have developed a standardized coding system for common cat behaviors. This allows for rapid documentation during observation sessions and facilitates later analysis. Key codes include:

Code Behavior Description
GRM Grooming Self-cleaning behaviors including licking, scratching, and fur manipulation
RST Resting Inactive periods in various positions (subtypes: RST-S for sleeping, RST-A for alert resting)
TRV Travel Purposeful movement between locations (notation includes direction and pace)
HNT Hunting Predatory behaviors including stalking, pouncing, and consumption of prey
SCI Social Interaction Interactions with other cats (subtypes indicate friendly, neutral, or aggressive)
HUM Human Interaction Interactions with humans (subtypes indicate initiated by cat or human)
TRM Territory Marking Scent marking, scratching surfaces, or other territorial behaviors
VOC Vocalization All vocal sounds (subtypes indicate meow, purr, hiss, etc.)
Research Tip: When conducting urban cat observations, position yourself where you have clear visibility but do not appear to be focusing directly on the cat. Many street cats are accustomed to human presence but may alter behavior if they perceive themselves as the object of sustained attention. I often pretend to read a book or newspaper while observing from peripheral vision.

Research Equipment

Effective feline research requires appropriate equipment that balances quality, portability, and unobtrusiveness. My field kit has evolved over the years as technology has advanced, but certain core items remain essential:

Field Documentation Tools
  • Leather-bound Field Journals - I use custom-made journals with water-resistant leather covers and high-quality paper that can withstand the variable Istanbul weather. Each journal is pre-labeled with page numbers and has a section for subject indices.
  • Drawing Implements - A set of drawing pencils (2H to 6B) for sketching cat appearances, postures, and territorial maps. Colored pencils for noting distinctive markings.
  • Portable Ink Pens - Waterproof black ink for permanent records, blue ink for temporary observations that may require revision.
  • Field Measurement Tools - Compact measuring tape and small ruler for documenting environmental features relevant to cat behavior (height of perches, width of pathways, etc.)
Optical Equipment
  • Nikon Compact Binoculars (8x25) - Essential for observing cats at a distance without disturbing their natural behavior. The compact size allows discreet usage in urban settings where larger binoculars might attract unwanted attention.
  • Kodak DC260 Digital Camera - Acquired in 1999, this 1.6 megapixel digital camera has revolutionized my documentation capabilities. Though expensive and somewhat bulky, it allows immediate verification of image quality and eliminates film development delays. NEW!
  • Traditional 35mm Camera with Zoom Lens - Still used for certain documentation where higher image quality is required. Film selection varies based on lighting conditions.
  • Small Handheld Mirror - Useful for indirect observation around corners or in confined spaces where direct line of sight is not possible.
My essential field research equipment laid out for documentation (December 2000)
Environmental Assessment Tools
  • Portable Thermometer - For recording ambient temperature during observations, critical for analyzing seasonal behavior variations.
  • Pocket Weather Meter - Measures wind speed and humidity, factors that significantly influence cat behavior and territory usage.
  • Sound Level Meter - Occasionally used to document ambient noise levels in different urban environments and correlate with cat stress behaviors.
  • Small Samples Kit - Plastic containers, tweezers, and labels for collecting environmental samples when relevant (fur samples, territorial marking residue, etc.)
Support Equipment
  • Field Satchel - Traditional Turkish leather satchel modified with additional compartments for organizing research equipment while maintaining an unobtrusive appearance.
  • Portable Seating - Collapsible lightweight stool for extended observation sessions in locations without suitable public seating.
  • Reference Materials - Laminated identification charts for quick reference to cat breed characteristics, common urban bird species (relevant for hunting behavior), and maps of study areas.
  • Battery-Powered Voice Recorder - For dictating observations when writing is impractical due to weather or lighting conditions.
Equipment Tip: I highly recommend investing in quality waterproof covers for all paper-based research materials. Istanbul's weather can change rapidly, particularly near the Bosphorus, and nothing is more devastating to a researcher than losing irreplaceable field notes to unexpected rain.

Community Interview Methodology

While direct observation forms the foundation of my research, the insights of local residents who interact with street cats daily provide invaluable contextual information and historical continuity. I have developed a structured approach to gathering this community knowledge:

Key Informant Network

Over the years, I have established relationships with a network of "key informants" across Istanbul's historic districts. These individuals include:

  • Shopkeepers - Particularly those with long-established businesses who can provide observations over extended periods
  • Religious Personnel - Mosque imams and caretakers who frequently interact with resident mosque cats
  • Market Vendors - Especially fish sellers and butchers who regularly provide food to neighborhood cats
  • Elderly Residents - Who can provide historical perspective on changes in cat populations and human attitudes
  • Municipal Workers - Street cleaners and maintenance staff who observe cats across different territories

For each key informant, I maintain a record card with contact information, areas of specific knowledge, and reliability assessment based on cross-verification of their reports with direct observations.

Semi-Structured Interview Protocol

When conducting formal interviews with community members, I use a semi-structured approach that balances consistent data collection with flexibility to pursue unique insights. My standard interview protocol includes:

  1. Background Establishment - Questions about the informant's history in the neighborhood, daily routines, and general relationship with local cats
  2. Specific Cat Identification - Using photographs or descriptions to confirm which specific cats the informant recognizes and interacts with
  3. Behavioral Observations - Structured questions about specific behavioral patterns, feeding habits, territorial boundaries, and social interactions
  4. Historical Information - Questions about changes observed over time, previous generations of cats, and evolving human-cat relationships
  5. Cultural Context - Exploration of local customs, beliefs, and practices related to cats in the specific neighborhood

All interviews are documented in dedicated interview journals, with direct quotations clearly distinguished from paraphrasing or my own interpretations. When permitted by the informant, I record audio of interviews for later review and more accurate quotation.

Data Triangulation

To ensure accuracy, I employ methodological triangulation by cross-referencing three sources of information:

  1. Direct Observation - My own documented field observations
  2. Primary Informant Accounts - Information from key community members
  3. Secondary Verification - Confirmation from additional informants or historical records

Information that cannot be verified through at least two independent sources is clearly marked as "unconfirmed" in my research notes and publications. This triangulation process is particularly important when documenting historical information or behaviors that occur infrequently.

"The shopkeepers and local residents are not merely sources of anecdotes about cats - they are essential co-researchers who provide continuity of observation that no single academic researcher could achieve alone. Their knowledge, when properly documented and verified, is as valuable as any laboratory finding."
- From my research journal, April 1996

Territorial Mapping Techniques

Understanding the spatial dimensions of feline life is crucial to my research. I have developed systematic mapping techniques to document territorial boundaries, movement patterns, and usage of urban space:

Base Map Preparation

For each study area, I prepare detailed base maps using multiple sources:

  • Official City Maps - Obtained from municipal offices and modified to include relevant microfeatures
  • Historical Maps - Particularly useful for identifying persistent landscape features that influence cat territories
  • Hand-Drawn Supplements - Detailed sketches of specific areas including features not represented on official maps

These base maps are transferred onto transparent overlays that allow for layering of different information categories. Each base map includes permanent features, cardinal directions, and a consistent scale notation.

Territorial Boundary Documentation

To establish the territories of individual cats, I employ a combination of techniques:

  1. Sighting Plotation - Recording the precise location of each cat sighting on the map with date and time notation
  2. Behavior Mapping - Documenting locations of specific territorial behaviors such as scent marking, confrontations with other cats, or confident relaxation
  3. Movement Tracking - When possible, I follow individual cats through their territories, mapping their complete movement paths and noting activities at each location
  4. Boundary Testing - Observing responses at presumed territory edges, particularly when other cats approach or cross apparent boundaries

After collecting sufficient data points (typically 20+ observations per cat over at least three months), I establish preliminary territory maps with core areas, buffer zones, and disputed boundaries clearly marked.

Resource and Feature Mapping

Beyond simple boundaries, my territorial maps document critical resources and features that influence cat behavior:

  • Food Sources - Regular feeding locations, hunting grounds, restaurant dumpsters, etc.
  • Water Sources - Natural and artificial water features accessible to cats
  • Shelter Sites - Sleeping locations, weather protection areas, and seasonal retreats
  • Vertical Features - Climbing structures, observation perches, and escape routes
  • Human Activity Zones - Areas of high, moderate, and low human presence
  • Hazard Areas - Locations of known dangers such as heavy traffic or aggressive dogs
