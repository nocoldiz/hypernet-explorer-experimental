Introduction: The Cats of Empire

Throughout the six centuries of Ottoman rule (1299-1922), cats held a position of remarkable privilege and cultural significance. Unlike many other civilizations where felines were merely tolerated as practical mousers, the Ottoman Empire embraced cats as integral members of society, worthy of respect, admiration, and even legal protection.

This special relationship was not accidental but stemmed from several complementary influences: Islamic teachings regarding animal welfare, pragmatic urban management concerns, and the aesthetic appreciation of feline grace that permeated Ottoman artistic sensibilities.

As I have documented through my decades of research, the legacy of this relationship continues in modern Istanbul, where street cats enjoy a status unparalleled in most global cities. To understand the present-day cat culture of Istanbul, one must first understand its deep Ottoman roots.

This chronicle attempts to provide a comprehensive overview of cat history throughout the Ottoman period, from palace felines who dined from golden dishes to the humble mosque cats who won the hearts of sultans. Through art, literature, architecture, and legal records, we can trace the remarkable story of this enduring cultural partnership.

Origins of Ottoman Cat Reverence

The Ottoman reverence for cats did not emerge in isolation but built upon several pre-existing cultural foundations:

Islamic Influence

The Prophet Muhammad's fondness for cats is well-documented in numerous hadith (sayings and actions attributed to the Prophet). Among the most beloved stories is that of Muezza, the Prophet's cat, whom he so favored that he once cut off the sleeve of his garment rather than disturb the sleeping feline. This established a precedent of respect for cats throughout the Islamic world.

Islamic jurisprudence further elevated the status of cats by declaring them to be "tahir" (ritually clean) animals, unlike dogs. This designation allowed cats to enter homes and even mosques freely, establishing them as acceptable domestic companions in a way that many other animals were not.

"If a cat passes in front of you while you are praying, it does not invalidate your prayer, for the cat is considered one of the people of the house."
- Attributed to the Prophet Muhammad

Byzantine Heritage

When the Ottomans conquered Constantinople in 1453, they inherited a city already populated with cats. The Byzantine Empire had its own tradition of feline appreciation, particularly in monasteries where cats were kept to protect valuable manuscripts from rodents. This existing cat population provided the foundation for the flourishing Ottoman cat culture that would develop over subsequent centuries.

Practical Urban Management

Beyond religious and cultural factors, the Ottomans recognized the practical value of cats in managing rodent populations in dense urban areas. Istanbul, as one of the world's largest cities during much of the Ottoman period, faced significant challenges in waste management and disease control. Cats served as natural pest controllers, helping to mitigate public health concerns in an era before modern sanitation.

Diagram illustrating how cats were integrated into Ottoman urban sanitation planning (from "Ottoman Urban Planning," 1994)

The Sultans and Their Cats

Throughout Ottoman history, many sultans were known for their particular affection for cats. Their personal examples reinforced the cultural significance of felines and often resulted in policies that benefited cats throughout the empire.

Sultan Mehmed II (The Conqueror)
Reign: 1444-1446, 1451-1481

Though primarily remembered for his conquest of Constantinople, Mehmed II was reportedly fond of cats and brought several feline companions with him during the siege of the Byzantine capital. Court records indicate that after establishing his rule, he issued specific orders for the feeding of street cats around the newly converted Hagia Sophia mosque.

A possibly apocryphal but widely repeated story claims that when planning the layout of the new Ottoman capital, Mehmed II instructed architects to design small openings at the base of buildings specifically to allow cats to move freely throughout the city.

Sultan Suleiman I (The Magnificent)
Reign: 1520-1566

During his long reign, Suleiman the Magnificent established several endowments (vakıf) specifically for the care of street cats in Istanbul. European ambassadors to his court reported that the Sultan would sometimes pause important state meetings to attend to a cat that had wandered into the audience chamber.

In his private correspondence, Suleiman wrote of a particularly favored white Angora cat named "Şeker" (Sugar), who enjoyed privileges that many human courtiers might envy, including a silk cushion in the imperial council chamber.

Ahmed III
Reign: 1703-1730

Ahmed III, a patron of the arts during the "Tulip Era," commissioned several portraits that included his favorite cats. He reportedly decreed that special ceramic bowls be placed throughout the Topkapı Palace gardens to provide water for cats, particularly during summer months.

During his reign, the court physician Ibrahim Hakki wrote one of the earliest known veterinary texts specifically addressing feline health, "Treatise on the Maladies of Palace Cats," suggesting the significant value placed on cat welfare in the imperial household.

Reconstruction of an imperial cat house from Topkapı Palace, featuring elaborate Ottoman architectural details (Ottoman Museum of Daily Life)

By the 19th century, the Ottoman sultans' affection for cats had become so well-known that it featured in diplomatic communications. British ambassador Stratford Canning noted in his memoirs that he once successfully softened Sultan Abdülmecid I's position on a difficult treaty negotiation by presenting him with a rare blue-eyed white cat from England.

Legal Status and Protection

Perhaps the most remarkable aspect of Ottoman cat culture was the formal legal protection extended to felines throughout the empire. Unlike many contemporary European societies where animals were generally considered property with few rights, Ottoman jurisprudence recognized a degree of legal standing for cats.

Vakıf (Endowment) System

The Ottoman vakıf (charitable endowment) system provided one of the primary mechanisms for institutionalizing cat care. Historical records from the 16th-18th centuries reveal numerous endowments specifically created to feed and shelter street cats. These endowments allocated funds, property, or food resources to ensure the welfare of urban cat populations.

One of the most well-documented examples is the Kedi Vakfı (Cat Endowment) established in 1658 near the Hagia Sophia, which designated revenues from several shops to be used exclusively for feeding the area's stray cats. This endowment continued to function until the early 20th century, demonstrating the remarkable longevity of Ottoman cat protection measures.

Punishment for Mistreatment

Ottoman court records (kadı registers) from various provinces contain numerous cases involving punishment for the mistreatment of cats. Unlike the often symbolic or nominal penalties assigned to animal cruelty in many historical legal systems, Ottoman courts frequently imposed significant fines or even corporal punishment for harming cats.

A court record from Bursa dated 1563 documents a case where a merchant was not only fined but temporarily banned from the marketplace for deliberately injuring a cat that had entered his shop - punishment that would have represented a serious financial and social consequence.

"If any soul shall harm or cause suffering to the cats of the mosque, let them face the qadi's judgment and make restitution for this offense against God's creatures and the peace of the sacred space."
- From deed of endowment for Süleymaniye Mosque, 1557

Architectural Accommodations

Ottoman building codes, particularly for public and religious structures, often included specific provisions for cat welfare. Many mosques, medreses (religious schools), and hans (caravanserais) featured purposely designed architectural elements to accommodate cats:

  • Kedi Kapısı (Cat Doors) - Small openings built into the walls of buildings to allow cats free passage
  • Kedi Evleri (Cat Houses) - Sheltered niches built into exterior walls to provide sleeping places
  • Kedi Çeşmesi (Cat Fountains) - Low water basins designed specifically for cats to drink from

These architectural features can still be observed in many historic Ottoman structures throughout Istanbul and other former Ottoman cities, providing physical evidence of institutionalized cat care.

Original kedi kapısı (cat door) at Zeyrek Mosque, dating to the 16th century

Mosque Cats: The Sacred Guardians

Among the most culturally significant felines in Ottoman society were the mosque cats, who occupied a unique status between wild and domesticated animals. Neither fully tame pets nor truly feral strays, these cats became integral to the functioning and atmosphere of Ottoman religious spaces.

Historical Development

The tradition of mosque cats appears to have developed gradually during the early Ottoman period. Initial practical considerations - cats keeping mosque libraries free from rodents that might damage valuable manuscripts - evolved into a more symbolic relationship where the cats were seen as contributing to the sanctity and purity of the space.

By the 16th century, major Ottoman mosques typically maintained a small population of resident cats who were cared for by mosque staff and local worshippers. Some larger complexes, such as the Süleymaniye Mosque, had designated caretakers responsible for feeding and monitoring the health of the mosque cat population.

Functions and Benefits

Mosque cats served multiple functions in Ottoman religious spaces:

  • Practical Protection - Guarding manuscripts, prayer rugs, and wooden structures from rodents
  • Symbolic Purification - As ritually clean animals, cats reinforced the mosque's status as a purified space
  • Community Focus - Caring for mosque cats became a form of collective charitable action for the neighborhood
  • Educational Role - Imams often used the treatment of mosque cats as examples in teaching mercy and compassion
"The cat who guards the house of Allah is itself guarded by angels."
- Sheikh Ali Efendi, imam of Fatih Mosque, 1722

Notable Mosque Cats

Certain mosque cats achieved individual fame and were remembered in historical records:

Musa of Ayasofya - A distinctive orange tabby who lived at Hagia Sophia during the late 18th century and reportedly learned to recognize the call to prayer, appearing in the courtyard precisely at prayer times.

The Seven Cats of Eyüp Sultan - A legendary group of cats who supposedly guarded the tomb of Abu Ayyub al-Ansari (a companion of the Prophet Muhammad) and were believed to be descendants of a cat brought from Medina.

Husrev of Fatih Mosque - A black cat mentioned in multiple 19th century travelers' accounts who would reportedly lead lost visitors to the main entrance of the mosque complex during foggy days.

Contemporary mosque cats in the courtyard of Süleymaniye Mosque, continuing a tradition spanning centuries

The tradition of mosque cats has continued uninterrupted from the Ottoman era to the present day, with many of Istanbul's historic mosques still home to their feline guardians. Modern imams and mosque staff continue to maintain feeding stations and shelters for their resident cats, preserving this unique aspect of Ottoman religious culture.

Cats in Ottoman Art and Literature

The Ottoman fascination with cats extended naturally into the empire's artistic and literary expressions. Unlike many other Islamic cultures where artistic depiction of living beings was more strictly limited, Ottoman art frequently featured cats in various contexts.

Miniature Painting

Cats appear frequently in Ottoman miniature paintings, particularly in scenes depicting palace life or literary narratives. The detail and care with which cats were rendered suggests the artists' familiarity with and appreciation for feline anatomy and behavior.

In the celebrated "Surname-i Hümayun" (Book of Imperial Festivities) created in the 16th century, cats can be observed in multiple festival scenes, sometimes as participants in the festivities rather than merely as background elements.

Detail from an Ottoman miniature showing palace cats participating in a celebration (circa 1582)

Poetry and Literature

Ottoman divan poetry, the sophisticated literary form of the court, frequently employed cats as poetic subjects or metaphors. The grace, independence, and mysterious nature of cats made them particularly suitable for the complex metaphorical system of Ottoman verse.

The 18th-century poet Nedim composed several gazels (lyric poems) dedicated to cats, including his famous lines comparing a lover's elusive nature to that of a pampered palace cat:

"Like the sultan's favored cat, you stretch languidly upon silk cushions,
Accepting my devotion as your natural due, yet giving nothing in return."
- Nedim, early 18th century

Beyond courtly literature, cats featured prominently in Ottoman folk tales and proverbs. The collection "Hikayat-ı Kedi" (Cat Stories) compiled in the 17th century contains dozens of fables and morality tales featuring feline protagonists, suggesting their central role in popular storytelling.

Decorative Arts

Cat motifs appeared frequently in Ottoman decorative arts, including:

  • Ceramic Tiles - Particularly in İznik production from the 16th-17th centuries
  • Textile Patterns - Stylized cats in carpets and silk fabrics
  • Calligraphy - Cat forms sometimes incorporated into decorative calligraphic designs
  • Metalwork - Cat figures on ceremonial objects and everyday items

A particularly fascinating artistic development was the creation of specialized ceramic water vessels for cats. These "kedi çeşmesi" (cat fountains) often featured decorative elements and inscriptions specifically addressing the feline users, demonstrating the integration of aesthetic values with practical cat care.

18th century ceramic water bowl designed for cats, featuring fish motifs and an inscription reading "For Allah's silent creatures" (Topkapı Palace Museum)

Ottoman Cat Breeds and Varieties

While the Ottoman Empire is not typically associated with systematic cat breeding in the modern sense, historical records indicate that certain distinctive feline types were recognized, valued, and to some extent selectively maintained during the Ottoman period.

The Van Cat

Perhaps the most distinctive native cat variety associated with Ottoman territories is the Van cat, originating in the Lake Van region of eastern Anatolia. These remarkable cats, known for their white coats with colored markings on the head and tail and frequently heterochromatic eyes (one blue, one amber), were highly prized in Ottoman elite circles.

Historical records indicate that Van cats were sometimes sent as diplomatic gifts to other rulers or presented to the sultan by eastern provincial governors. Their unusual coloration and reported fondness for swimming made them objects of fascination in the imperial court.

Traditional Van cat with characteristic white coat and auburn head markings, similar to those prized in the Ottoman court

The Angora Cat

The most internationally renowned cat type associated with the Ottoman Empire is undoubtedly the Angora, named after Ankara (historically Angora), the central Anatolian city. With their silky, medium-length white coats and elegant bearing, Angora cats were highly valued within Ottoman society and eventually became one of the empire's most distinctive animal exports.

By the 17th century, Angora cats had become fashionable diplomatic gifts, with the Ottoman court sending them to European monarchs. Court records indicate that specialized caretakers were assigned to maintain the quality of Angora cats kept in the imperial household, suggesting an early form of selective breeding.

European visitors to the Ottoman Empire frequently commented on these distinctive cats. The French naturalist Pierre Belon noted in his 1553 travel account: "The cats of Angora are valued above all others for their silken fur, which is whiter than snow and soft as the finest wool."

Palace Cat Management

While not engaging in breeding programs in the modern sense, the Ottoman palace did maintain what might be considered a primitive form of cat selection. Court records from the 17th-18th centuries mention "kedi ağası" (cat stewards) whose responsibilities included:

  • Identifying and acquiring cats with desirable qualities for the palace
  • Maintaining records of notable palace cats and their offspring
  • Ensuring proper nutrition for cats of special significance
  • Selecting appropriate cats for diplomatic gifts

This level of organized attention to cat lineages, while far from modern breeding standards, suggests a recognition of hereditary traits and a desire to maintain certain feline characteristics within the palace population.

The Ottoman Cat Legacy Today

Though the Ottoman Empire officially ended in 1922, its distinctive cat culture has persisted into the modern era, particularly in Istanbul. This enduring legacy can be observed in several dimensions:

Cultural Continuity

Despite massive political, social, and cultural changes in Turkey throughout the 20th century, the special relationship between Istanbul residents and street cats has remained remarkably consistent with Ottoman-era attitudes. The modern Turkish approach to urban cats - neither fully domesticated nor treated as pests - directly continues the Ottoman middle path of respectful coexistence.

Local government policies regarding street cats in Istanbul often echo Ottoman-era protections, with municipal feeding programs and veterinary services replacing the historical vakıf system but serving a similar function of institutional care.

Modern municipal cat shelter in Istanbul's Fatih district, built in traditional Ottoman architectural style (2000)

Architectural Heritage

Many Ottoman-era architectural accommodations for cats remain functional in Istanbul's historic districts. Original cat doors, feeding niches, and water features built during the 16th-19th centuries continue to serve their intended purpose for today's street cats.

This physical continuity creates a remarkable living connection to the Ottoman past, with cats utilizing structures designed for their ancestors hundreds of years earlier. When restoration work is conducted on historic buildings, these feline-specific features are typically preserved as important cultural heritage elements.

Religious Continuity

The Islamic foundations of Ottoman cat reverence continue to influence contemporary attitudes. Many Istanbul mosques maintain the tradition of resident cats, and imams frequently incorporate messages about compassionate treatment of cats into their teachings, directly continuing Ottoman religious practices.

During my research, I have documented numerous instances of mosque administrators using dedicated funds for cat care, echoing the historical vakıf system of the Ottoman era. As recently as 2000, the historic Nuruosmaniye Mosque established a formal cat welfare program explicitly referencing Ottoman precedents.

International Influence

The Ottoman cat tradition has influenced global cat culture in several ways:

  • The worldwide popularity of Turkish cat breeds (particularly the Turkish Angora and Turkish Van)
  • The concept of "mosque cats" that has become a distinctive cultural feature recognized internationally
  • Istanbul's reputation as a "cat city" that attracts tourists specifically interested in observing the cat culture

In my assessment as a historian of Ottoman feline culture, the cat-human relationship that evolved during the Ottoman period represents one of the empire's most successful and enduring cultural legacies - one that continues to evolve while maintaining its core characteristics into the 21st century.

In collaboration with the Istanbul University Rare Manuscripts Division, I am working to translate several previously unstudied Ottoman texts dealing with cats, including:

  • "Tedbir-i Kedi" (Management of Cats) - An 18th century text on palace cat maintenance
  • "Hikayat-ı Kediler" (Stories of Cats) - A collection of cat-centered folk tales from various Ottoman provinces
