Welcome to the Primate Photography Archive

Latest Update: March 15, 2001 - Added new photographs from Madagascar expedition

This site documents fourteen years of field photography studying primate behavior in natural habitats across Africa, South America, and Madagascar. From 1987 to 2001, I have captured over 3,000 photographs of various monkey and ape species in their natural environments.

Recent Expeditions

Madagascar - February 2001
Documented ring-tailed lemurs and sifakas in Andasibe-Mantadia National Park. Captured rare feeding behaviors during fruit season.

Costa Rica - September 2000
Extended study of howler monkey territorial displays. First photographic evidence of inter-troop boundary negotiations.

Cameroon - January 2000
Three-month documentation of mandrill social structures in dense rainforest conditions.

Field Research Notes

Tool Use in New World Primates (1999)

Systematic documentation of capuchin monkey tool use reveals sophisticated problem-solving abilities previously underestimated. Over 200 hours of observation in Panamanian forests yielded 47 distinct tool-use episodes, including modification of sticks for termite extraction and selection of stones based on hardness for nut-cracking.

Most significant finding: juveniles learn tool techniques through direct observation and practice, not trial-and-error. This suggests cultural transmission of knowledge within troops, similar to patterns observed in African chimpanzees.

Nocturnal Behavior Patterns in Lemuriformes (2001)

Three-month study using infrared photography revealed previously unknown nighttime social interactions among ring-tailed lemurs. Contrary to established literature describing them as primarily diurnal, significant activity occurs during full moon periods.

Observed behaviors include territory marking expeditions, inter-group scent communication, and what appears to be recreational play among adults. These findings challenge assumptions about energy conservation strategies in Madagascar's harsh environment.

Facial Expression Analysis in Old World Monkeys (1995)

Photographic analysis of over 800 facial expressions across six species reveals universal signals for aggression, submission, and play invitation. However, species-specific variations exist, particularly in lip positioning and eye contact duration.

Implications for understanding primate communication evolution and potential applications to captive animal welfare assessment.
